ISO 14971 Risk Management Framework — Paraphrased Reference Knowledge Base
==========================================================================
NOTE: This document is a paraphrased educational summary for AI-assisted RMF
generation. It is not a copy of the ISO 14971 standard. All content has been
reworded and reorganised for use as a language model context document.
==========================================================================


1. SCOPE AND LIFECYCLE COVERAGE
--------------------------------
Risk management under ISO 14971 must cover the entire product lifecycle:
concept and design, development and verification, manufacturing and production,
installation and commissioning, clinical use and operation, maintenance and
servicing, and decommissioning or disposal. All activities that could affect the
safety of the device or its users must be considered throughout this lifecycle.
Risk management is not a one-time activity — it must be continuously maintained
and updated as new information becomes available.


2. RISK MANAGEMENT PLAN
------------------------
Before risk analysis begins, a Risk Management Plan (RMP) must be established.
The RMP defines: the scope of risk management activities; the responsibilities
and required competencies of personnel involved; the criteria for determining
risk acceptability; the methods and tools used for hazard identification, risk
estimation, and risk evaluation; the approach for reviewing risk management
activities before product release; and the methods for collecting and reviewing
post-production information. The RMP must be documented and approved before
risk analysis work commences.


3. INTENDED USE AND REASONABLY FORESEEABLE MISUSE
---------------------------------------------------
Both the intended use and reasonably foreseeable misuse scenarios must be
identified and documented. Intended use describes the clinical purpose, patient
population, intended user, and operating environment of the device.
Foreseeable misuse includes using the device outside its intended population
(e.g., paediatric use of an adult device), use in unvalidated environments,
improper storage, handling errors, maintenance neglect, and use after the end
of device service life. The characteristics of the intended users — including
skill level, training, and cognitive load — must be considered, particularly
for lay users and home-use devices.


4. HAZARD IDENTIFICATION
-------------------------
A hazard is a potential source of harm. Hazard identification must be systematic
and comprehensive, considering:

  - Energy-related hazards: electrical, mechanical, thermal, acoustic, radiation
  - Biological hazards: biocompatibility, infection, sterility
  - Chemical hazards: toxicity, drug interactions, leachables and extractables
  - Software and algorithm hazards: incorrect outputs, software failures, cybersecurity
  - Human factors hazards: use errors, misinterpretation of displays, alarm fatigue
  - Environmental hazards: temperature extremes, humidity, electromagnetic interference
  - Connectivity and data hazards: communication failures, data corruption, latency
  - Alarm system hazards: false alarms, missed alarms, alarm fatigue

Hazard identification should draw on historical incident data, literature
review, FMEA (Failure Modes and Effects Analysis), FTA (Fault Tree Analysis),
expert judgment, and usability analysis. Both hardware and software failure
modes should be considered, including single-fault conditions.


5. SEQUENCE OF EVENTS AND HAZARDOUS SITUATIONS
------------------------------------------------
For each identified hazard, the foreseeable sequences of events that lead from
the hazard to a hazardous situation must be described. A hazardous situation
arises when a person (patient, user, or bystander) is exposed to a hazard. The
transition from hazardous situation to harm depends on whether there is an
opportunity for the person to recognise and avoid the harm. Both normal-use
scenarios and foreseeable misuse scenarios must be considered in identifying
hazardous situations. Multiple sequences of events may lead from a single hazard
to different hazardous situations.


6. RISK ESTIMATION — SEVERITY AND PROBABILITY SCALES
------------------------------------------------------
Risk is estimated by combining the severity of potential harm with the
probability that the harm will occur.

SEVERITY LEVELS (from least to most severe):
  - Negligible: No injury or impact on health; temporary discomfort at most
  - Minor:      Reversible, temporary injury; no medical intervention required
  - Serious:    Injury requiring medical intervention; temporary disability
  - Critical:   Irreversible injury; permanent impairment or significant disability
  - Catastrophic: Death or life-threatening injury

PROBABILITY LEVELS (from least to most frequent):
  - Remote:      Very unlikely to occur in the device's expected service life
  - Occasional:  Unlikely but possible; may occur in a small proportion of uses
  - Probable:    Likely to occur in a significant proportion of uses
  - Frequent:    Expected to occur regularly in normal use conditions

Risk estimation should consider both: (a) the probability of the hazardous
situation occurring, and (b) the conditional probability that the hazardous
situation results in harm given that it occurs.

RISK LEVEL MATRIX (indicative):
  Catastrophic  + Occasional/Probable/Frequent = HIGH
  Catastrophic  + Remote                       = HIGH (benefit-risk required)
  Critical      + Probable/Frequent            = HIGH
  Critical      + Occasional                   = HIGH
  Critical      + Remote                       = MEDIUM
  Serious       + Probable/Frequent            = HIGH
  Serious       + Occasional                   = MEDIUM
  Serious       + Remote                       = LOW
  Minor         + Probable/Frequent            = MEDIUM
  Minor         + Occasional/Remote            = LOW
  Negligible    + Any                          = LOW


7. RISK EVALUATION
-------------------
After estimating the risk, it must be compared against the risk acceptability
criteria defined in the RMP. If a risk is unacceptable, risk control measures
must be implemented. If a risk falls into a broadly acceptable category, it
still requires documentation and monitoring. Risks in the ALARP (As Low As
Reasonably Practicable) region require justification that further risk reduction
is impracticable or that the residual risk is outweighed by clinical benefit.


8. RISK CONTROL — PRIORITY ORDER
----------------------------------
Risk control must follow a strict priority order:

  Priority 1 — Inherent safety by design:
    Eliminate or reduce the hazard or hazardous situation through design
    choices. Examples: fail-safe mechanisms, hardware interlocks, physical
    separation of hazardous components, use of inherently safer materials.
    This is always preferred over other control strategies.

  Priority 2 — Protective measures in the device or manufacturing process:
    Add protective features that reduce the risk but do not eliminate the
    hazard. Examples: audible and visual alarms, automatic shut-off, software
    dose limits, secondary confirmation steps, tamper-evident packaging.
    Protective measures should be automatic and not dependent on user action.

  Priority 3 — Information for safety:
    Address residual risks through warnings, labeling, instructions for use,
    training programs, and contraindications. This is the least effective
    control strategy and should not be used as the primary or sole control
    for significant risks. Information for safety is appropriate as a
    supplement to higher-priority controls.

Any risk control measure must not introduce new hazards or increase existing
risks. A risk-risk tradeoff analysis may be required when controls have
secondary effects.


9. VERIFICATION OF RISK CONTROL MEASURES
------------------------------------------
Each risk control measure must be verified for:
  (a) Correct implementation: the measure has been implemented as intended
  (b) Effectiveness: the measure actually reduces risk to the expected level
  (c) No new hazards introduced: the measure does not create additional risks

Verification methods should be specific, measurable, and documented. Appropriate
methods include: design review, analysis, bench testing, electrical safety
testing, software testing, simulated clinical use testing, usability evaluation
(formative and summative), analytical performance testing, and clinical study.
Results of verification must be recorded in the Risk Management File.


10. RESIDUAL RISK EVALUATION
------------------------------
After all risk controls are applied, the risk for each hazardous situation must
be re-evaluated using the same risk estimation criteria. This re-evaluated risk
is the residual risk. If the residual risk is still unacceptable, the risk
control process must be repeated or a benefit-risk analysis must be documented.
Residual severity and probability should both be re-estimated after considering
the effectiveness of implemented controls.


11. BENEFIT-RISK ANALYSIS
---------------------------
When residual risk cannot be reduced further by practicable risk controls, the
clinical benefits of the device must be weighed against the remaining risks.
This benefit-risk analysis must consider: the clinical benefit of the intended
use; the probability of clinical benefit occurring; alternative therapies
available to the patient; and the severity and probability of the residual
risks. If residual risks are not justified by benefits, the device should not
be released. The benefit-risk analysis must be documented and retained in the
Risk Management File.


12. OVERALL RESIDUAL RISK
---------------------------
The cumulative effect of all residual risks from the entire risk analysis must
be evaluated as an overall residual risk. This assessment considers whether
the combination of all individual residual risks — even if each is acceptable
in isolation — is acceptable as a whole. This overall evaluation must be
documented, reviewed, and approved before the device is released for production
or placed on the market.


13. PRODUCTION AND POST-PRODUCTION INFORMATION
------------------------------------------------
After market release, information from production, field use, customer
complaints, adverse event reports, vigilance databases, post-market surveillance,
post-market clinical follow-up, published literature, and recall data must be
systematically collected and reviewed. If new hazards are identified, risk
estimates change, or new risk controls are needed, the Risk Management File
must be updated. Traceability between post-production information and risk
management activities must be maintained.


14. RISK MANAGEMENT FILE AND TRACEABILITY
------------------------------------------
The Risk Management File (RMF) must maintain full traceability across:
  - Hazard identification
  → Sequence of events leading to hazardous situation
  → Hazardous situation description
  → Possible harm and severity
  → Initial risk level (probability × severity)
  → Risk control measures applied (in priority order)
  → Verification method and evidence for each control
  → Residual risk level
  → Residual risk acceptability assessment or benefit-risk justification
  → Overall residual risk conclusion
  → Review and release status

Each entry in the RMF must be traceable to the hazard it addresses, the
controls applied, and the evidence supporting the risk evaluation conclusion.

DEVICE-SPECIFIC RISK CONSIDERATIONS
==========================================================================


INFUSION PUMP SYSTEMS (Volumetric, Syringe, Ambulatory):
---------------------------------------------------------
Critical safety functions that must be addressed in every infusion pump RMF:

Free-flow protection:
  Anti-free-flow (AFF) mechanisms must be a mandatory hardware design feature.
  Free-flow from gravity is potentially fatal for high-alert medications.
  AFF clamp design must be validated for ease of engagement under clinical
  conditions including gloved hands and time pressure.

Occlusion management:
  Both upstream and downstream occlusion detection must be validated. Alarm
  threshold sensitivity directly affects downstream drug delivery accuracy
  and the risk of dose stacking after occlusion clearance. The accumulated
  volume at the time of occlusion must be considered in the clinical protocol.

Air-in-line detection:
  Ultrasonic or optical air-in-line detectors must be validated to detect
  clinically relevant bubble volumes per IEC 60601-2-24. Empty-bag detection
  and automatic pump stop prevent late-stage air entry. Priming procedures
  must be clearly described in the IFU.

Drug library and Dose Error Reduction Software (DERS):
  Drug library validation must confirm that drug profiles, concentration units,
  and hard/soft limit values are clinically correct. Hard limits must prevent
  activation when a dose exceeds the maximum safe threshold. Dose calculation
  software must be validated per IEC 62304 and IEC 60601-2-24.

Battery management:
  Battery capacity must be validated for the minimum required transport
  duration under maximum clinical load. Multi-stage low battery alarms with
  audible and visual indicators at ≥30 minutes remaining capacity are required.
  Battery state must be verifiable before patient transport.

Alarm system:
  Alarms must be audible, visible, and prioritised per IEC 60601-1-8.
  High-priority alarms (e.g., air-in-line, occlusion, free-flow) must be
  distinct from low-priority alarms. Non-defeatable alarm escalation for
  critical events must be implemented. Alarm design must consider the high-
  alarm-frequency clinical environment and the known risks of alarm fatigue.

Human factors and usability:
  Touchscreen design must be validated under clinical use conditions including
  gloves, time pressure, and interruptions. Programming workflows must be
  evaluated in summative usability studies per IEC 62366. Confirmation steps
  must be required before starting high-alert drug infusions.

Software reliability:
  Software must be classified and developed per IEC 62304. Fail-safe behaviour
  on software fault (watchdog timer, controlled stop, alarm) must be designed
  and validated. Software regression testing is required before each release.

Applicable standards: IEC 60601-2-24, IEC 60601-1-8, IEC 62304, IEC 62366,
IEC 60601-1, applicable drug library guideline (e.g., ECRI, ISMP).


BLOOD GLUCOSE MONITORING SYSTEMS (Self-Monitoring, POC):
---------------------------------------------------------
Analytical accuracy requirements:
  Measurement accuracy must meet ISO 15197 requirements: ≥95% of results
  within ±15 mg/dL of reference for results <100 mg/dL, or within ±15% for
  results ≥100 mg/dL. Clinical accuracy criteria (e.g., Clarke Error Grid)
  must also be assessed. Strip lot-to-lot variability must be characterised
  and controlled through lot-release QC specifications.

Interference and hematocrit effects:
  Interference from commonly encountered substances (acetaminophen, vitamin C,
  uric acid, maltose, ibuprofen) must be characterised per ISO 15197 Annex C.
  Hematocrit range must be validated (typically 20–60% Hct). Out-of-range
  hematocrit should be flagged by the device. Interference test results must
  be documented in the IFU.

Strip integrity and environmental stability:
  Strip stability must be validated across the stated operating temperature
  and humidity range. Open-vial in-use stability must be established (typically
  90 days after opening). Lot expiry detection via barcode or chip is strongly
  recommended. Storage and environmental conditions must be clearly labelled.

Sample volume and fill detection:
  Insufficient sample volume must be detected before a result is displayed.
  Fill detection algorithms must block result display and provide an error code
  when sample volume is below the validated minimum. Audible or visual fill
  confirmation feedback assists the user.

User interface and display:
  Result display must be legible under typical ambient light conditions.
  Minimum digit height and contrast ratios must meet usability requirements
  per IEC 62366. Units of measurement (mmol/L or mg/dL) must be prominently
  displayed alongside every result. Automatic backlight activation in low-
  light conditions is recommended.

Hypoglycaemia and hyperglycaemia alerting:
  Threshold alarms for critical high and low glucose values must be
  distinguishable from routine result displays. Audible alarms for HI/LO
  conditions must be clearly described in the IFU. Users must be clearly
  instructed on when to seek emergency medical care.

Auto-coding or no-coding:
  Manual strip coding is a known source of systematic error. Auto-coding via
  barcode or strip chip eliminates this error mode and is strongly preferred.
  If manual coding is required, a mismatch alarm must be implemented.

Data connectivity:
  Bluetooth or NFC data transmission to companion apps must be validated for
  data integrity. CRC or checksum validation must prevent incorrect values
  from being displayed in the app. All transmitted values must match meter
  display values within the device specification.

Lay user considerations:
  Self-monitoring devices are used by patients with minimal medical training.
  Formative and summative usability evaluations per IEC 62366 are required
  with the intended lay user population. Plain-language output, clear action
  guidance for critical results, and accessible packaging are required. Elderly
  users, visually impaired users, and users with manual dexterity limitations
  must be considered.

Applicable standards: ISO 15197, IEC 62366, IEC 60601-1, applicable regional
IVD regulations (EU IVDR, FDA 21 CFR Part 820), relevant CLSI guidelines.
